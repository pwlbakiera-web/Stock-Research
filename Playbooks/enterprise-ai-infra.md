# Playbook: Enterprise IT / AI Infrastructure (Servers, Networking, Storage, As-a-Service)

Covers infrastructure vendors selling into enterprise and AI workloads: servers/AI systems, networking, storage, and hybrid-cloud/as-a-service models. Relevant names: Dell, HPE, Cisco, Arista, Super Micro, Juniper, Lenovo.

## Key operating KPIs

- **AI systems orders / bookings / backlog** — quarterly and cumulative figures, plus backlog entering the quarter. Backlog is not revenue — it's often supply-gated. Check product backlog vs. service backlog separately.
- **AI customer concentration** — sovereign, neocloud, and hyperscaler buyers are often just one or two names, creating lumpy-revenue risk. This is frequently undisclosed — flag it when you can't find it.
- **Units vs. ASP** — critical: split revenue growth into volume vs. price. During a memory up-cycle, revenue growth is often mostly ASP pass-through (DRAM/NAND pricing), not real unit growth. Diagnostic question: "how much are units up vs. revenue?"
- **ARR / recurring revenue (as-a-service models)** — value, growth rate, net retention (>100% is healthy), customer count, managed systems under contract.
- **Segment mix** — share of higher-margin networking vs. lower-margin server revenue; mix shift is the main driver of multiple re-rating.
- **Market share** — Ethernet switching, AI-optimized servers. Direction matters more than level: gaining share vs. defending it.

## Unit economics / Fermi drivers

- **Unit of analysis:** AI server / rack / GPU system / Ethernet port / storage system.
- **Revenue bridge:** units × ASP; always split growth into volume vs. ASP, since memory/GPU ASP can be pure pass-through rather than value capture.
- **Cost/unit:** bill of materials (GPU, DRAM, NAND, networking), integration, warranty/service, working capital.
- **Utilization/frequency:** backlog conversion speed, supply availability, customer deployment cadence.
- **Mature margin anchor:** segment operating margin — networking as the high-margin anchor, servers as the lower-margin volume business; EV/Sales is only a secondary check.
- **Sensitivity variables:** gross margin per AI system, units vs. ASP, customer concentration, memory price cycle, backlog duration.
- For AI-server growth stories, a Fermi-style build (units × price × margin, discounted from a mature-margin base) is more informative than headline revenue growth — revenue growth without margin capture is a weak bet.

## Key financial KPIs

- **Operating margin per segment** — networking at 20%+ signals quality; server/AI-server at 5–15% is low. Blended margin depends heavily on mix.
- **Gross margin trend** — component/memory cost pressure; memory alone can be over 50% of a server's bill of materials. Falling gross margin despite rising revenue is a warning sign.
- **AI-systems gross margin** — often undisclosed; enterprise/sovereign deals typically carry higher margin than service-provider/neocloud deals. Flag if not broken out.
- **FCF and SBC-adjusted FCF** — capex is relatively low (asset-light vs. running fabs), but working capital can swing significantly during an AI ramp.
- **Net leverage (net debt/EBITDA)** — matters after large acquisitions; a clear deleveraging path signals capital discipline.
- **Cost-savings programs** — distinguish one-time from structural; margin expansion built purely on cost cuts has a finite runway.

## Valuation metrics (sector-specific)

- Typically a later-phase business (operating leverage / capital return) → forward P/E and EV/EBITDA are primary; PEG relative to EPS growth.
- Apply a cyclical modifier almost always — server/AI capex demand is cyclical, and in a memory-shortage era earnings can be near a cyclical peak. **Value on mid-cycle/normalized earnings, never on peak.** Cheap on peak earnings can be expensive on a normalized basis.
- Reverse DCF — back out implied growth and compare it to guidance/the company's own framework. If implied ≈ actual, the stock is fair with no asymmetry.
- FCF yield vs. its own history and vs. the long-run average.
- EV/Sales is of limited use for low-margin hardware — treat it as a comparison tool only, not a standalone verdict.

## Sector-specific red flags

- Revenue growth driven by ASP (memory pricing) rather than volume ("units up only slightly") — cyclical and not durable.
- "Record" backlog disclosed without a dollar figure or without customer-concentration context.
- AI servers sold as low-value-add GPU pass-through — revenue rises, margin doesn't.
- Margin expansion built on cost cuts rather than mix or demand (finite runway).
- High leverage combined with large acquisition-related goodwill (impairment risk).
- Networking organic growth trailing the fastest-growing competitor by a wide margin — a share-defender, not a share-gainer, in AI fabric.
- Insiders selling / no open-market buying during a hot momentum period — not a contrarian setup.

## Sources / benchmarks

- Competitive set for AI servers: Dell, Super Micro (watch gross-margin quality and any governance/legal overhang); competitive set for networking: Cisco, Arista.
- Memory-cycle read-through from upstream memory/storage suppliers is a useful durability check for server-vendor theses — the same DRAM/NAND cycle drives both.
- IDC Ethernet switch market data; Gartner data-center switching rankings.
- Any specific figures cited during an analysis (order backlogs, market-share points, segment margins) should be treated as calibration snapshots — always re-verify against the company's most recent filings before relying on them.
